The Big Bang and Cosmic Expansion

alors le big bang ce n'est pas sûr

The Big Bang theory represents our current best understanding of how the universe began and evolved into what we observe today. According to this model, the universe started from an extremely hot, dense state approximately 13.8 billion years ago.

The Initial Singularity

At the very beginning, all matter and energy in the universe was compressed into a point of infinite density called a singularity. At this moment, the fundamental forces of physics as we know them did not yet exist, and our current understanding of space and time breaks down.

The First Moments of Expansion

Rather than an explosion into space, the Big Bang was an expansion of space itself. In the first fraction of a second:

  • The universe underwent rapid inflation, expanding faster than the speed of light
  • Temperatures were incredibly high, preventing the formation of atoms
  • Only fundamental particles and energy existed in this primordial soup

Cooling and Structure Formation

As the universe continued to expand, it began to cool:

  • Protons and neutrons formed from quarks
  • Light elements like hydrogen and helium were created through nucleosynthesis
  • After about 380,000 years, the universe cooled enough for electrons to combine with nuclei, forming the first atoms
  • This allowed light to travel freely for the first time, creating what we now observe as the cosmic microwave background radiation

Evidence for Expansion

The expansion theory is supported by several key observations:

  • Hubble's Law: Distant galaxies are moving away from us, with more distant galaxies receding faster
  • Cosmic microwave background: The afterglow of the Big Bang fills the entire universe
  • Abundance of light elements: The observed ratios of hydrogen and helium match theoretical predictions

This expansion continues today, with the universe growing larger and cooler as time progresses.
